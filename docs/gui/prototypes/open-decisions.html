Datum GUI · design decisions

Two open decisions, made visible

You asked for a picture instead of jargon. Here's what each choice actually looks like on the board — pick one per decision and I'll lock it into the design spec.

1 Where should the AI live?

When Datum's AI suggests a change — say, routing a net — where does that suggestion appear? Two very different products follow from the answer.

Dock (A) is familiar and conversational — but it's "a chat app bolted onto the shell," and it pulls your eye off the board into a side panel.

Overlay (B) puts the AI inside the design, not beside it: you see the actual proposed geometry in place and accept or reject it with one key. It never blocks you and never steals the canvas.

Your Design Book already argues for B (inline overlay) — "never a chat app bolted into the shell." The current prototype shows A (the bottom dock). This decision picks which one Datum commits to.

2 Context menus (right-click)

A context menu is the little menu that pops up when you right-click something — the fast way to act on whatever's under your cursor without traveling up to the menu bar.

Why it's an "open decision": Datum's docs specify the top menu bar and the command palette — but never the right-click menus. In KiCad and Altium a huge share of real editing happens by right-clicking, so this is a core interaction surface that's simply undesigned and unresearched.

The decision isn't whether — it must exist. It's designing what's in each one: the actions per object type (component vs. track vs. net vs. pad), the empty-canvas menu vs. the on-selection menu, and how they relate to the palette and menu bar.

Recommendation: a focused context-menu research pass across Altium / KiCad / Horizon first (the existing research skipped it), then design it into the interaction spec. It's the one gap that blocks the editor from feeling finished.
